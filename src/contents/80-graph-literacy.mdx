This trend from Gapminder[21] for the years 1978--2016, show the latest data from UNESCO[2]. Literacy is hard to define and measure. UNESCO[2] collected data from national surveys and censuses, all using slightly different definitions and methods for measuring literacy. The numbers between 1820 and 1960 are from [van Zanden[3]](http://gapm.io/x-zanoecd), who gathered the data from multiple sources, described in the chapter [Education since 1820](https://read.oecd-ilibrary.org/economics/how-was-life_9789264214262-en#page89) by Bas van Leeuwen and Jieli van Leeuwen-Li (see p. 94). The exact levels are even more uncertain than after 1970. We removed dips and peaks in [the trend line](https://read.oecd-ilibrary.org/economics/how-was-life_9789264214262-en#page96) from van Zanden[3] as they might give an impression of non-existing accuracy. Visit [Literacy rate---v1](http://gapm.io/dliterae) for detailed documentation of the data sources behind this graph.
